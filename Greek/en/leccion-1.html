1. Historical Context: Koine Greek

The New Testament was written in κοινή (koiné), which means ‘common’ or ‘universal’. This form of Greek became the lingua franca of the eastern Mediterranean from the conquests of Alexander the Great (3rd century BC) until the 4th century AD.

Key fact: Alexander the Great spread Greek culture and language across Egypt, Persia, and India. After his death, Koine Greek remained the language of trade, government, and education.

NT Koine Greek is not the pure Attic of Plato, but neither is it vulgar. It is a living, flexible, and precise language, ideal for communicating the gospel to all nations.

QUESTION: What are the three periods of Greek, and in which one was the NT written?
✓ Answer: Classical (800–300 BC), Koine (300 BC–400 AD), Modern (400 AD–present). The NT was written in Koine Greek.

Why Greek instead of Hebrew or Aramaic?

  • Universality: Koine Greek was the lingua franca of the eastern Roman Empire.
  • Mission to the Gentiles: The early church expanded rapidly among non-Jews who spoke Greek.
  • The Septuagint (LXX): The Greek translation of the OT was already the Bible of Hellenistic synagogues.
  • Jewish Diaspora: Jews scattered around the Mediterranean used Greek as their everyday language.
Koine enabled the gospel to reach people of all cultures without immediate translation. The LXX (Greek OT) was a providential tool for NT writers, who quote extensively from it.
2. Greek as an Inflected Language and the Five Cases

Unlike Spanish, which is analytic (uses prepositions and word order to indicate grammatical function), Greek is inflectional or synthetic: a single word changes its ending to show its function in the sentence.

Comparative example:
Spanish: “del hombre” (preposition + noun)
Greek: ἀνθρώπου (one word, genitive)

The five Greek cases

Nominative
Subject
ὁ λόγος (the word)
Genitive
Possession / origin
τοῦ λόγου (of the word)
Dative
Indirect object / instrument
τῷ λόγῳ (to the word)
Accusative
Direct object
τὸν λόγον (the word as object)
Vocative
Direct address
λόγε (O word!)

In Greek, word order does not determine function. For example, ὁ θεὸς ἀγαπᾷ τὸν κόσμον means “God loves the world,” but if we invert the order (τὸν κόσμον ἀγαπᾷ ὁ θεός) it still means “God loves the world,” because the cases tell us who is the subject and who is the object.

Exercise: Identify the case of the following words (use the article as a clue):
- τοῦ ἀνθρώπου → genitive (“of the man”)
- τὴν βασιλείαν → accusative (“the kingdom” as object)
- κύριε → vocative (“O Lord!”)
3. NT Manuscripts and Textual Criticism

The text of the Greek NT has been transmitted through thousands of manuscripts. The main types are:

  • Papyri (P): the oldest (2nd–4th century AD). E.g., P52 (fragment of John, c. AD 125).
  • Uncials (majuscules): parchment with capital letters (4th–10th century). E.g., Codex Vaticanus (B), Codex Sinaiticus (ℵ).
  • Minuscules: cursive script (9th century onward).
  • Lectionaries: books of liturgical readings.

Modern editions of the Greek NT, such as Nestle-Aland (NA28) and United Bible Societies (UBS5), reconstruct the original text by comparing all these sources and applying principles of textual criticism.

What is the practical difference between NA28 and UBS5?
Both have the same Greek text. NA28 includes a full critical apparatus (for scholars), while UBS5 has a selective apparatus geared toward translators, with a certainty rating (A, B, C, D) for each textual variant.

5. Connectives, Prepositions, and Negation

καί (and, also) and δέ (but, and, now) are the most important connectives. καί joins elements additively; δέ introduces contrast or narrative transition.

Key prepositions:
ἐν (in, within) + dative → position
εἰς (to, into, for) + accusative → direction
ἐκ/ἐξ (from, out of) + genitive → origin
ἀπό (from, away from) + genitive → separation

Negation: οὐ / οὐκ (not). οὐκ is used before a vowel, οὐ before a consonant.

The particle ὅτι: means “that” (declarative) or “because” (causal).

Translate: ἐν τῷ κόσμῳ → “in the world”
εἰς τὴν ἐκκλησίαν → “into the church”
ἐξ οὐρανοῦ → “from heaven”
6. Contextual NT Readings

John 1:1 – The Prologue of the Word

Ἐν ἀρχῇ ἦν ὁ λόγος, καὶ ὁ λόγος ἦν πρὸς τὸν θεόν, καὶ θεὸς ἦν ὁ λόγος.

Literal translation: “In (the) beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and God was the Word.”
Words you already know: ἐν, ἀρχῇ (beginning, dative), ἦν (was), ὁ (the), λόγος (Word), καί (and), πρός (with), τόν (the accusative), θεόν (God accusative), θεός (God nominative).
Theological note: The absence of the article in θεός (third clause) emphasises the quality of divinity of the Word, without identifying him completely with the Father (“the Word was divine” or “was God by nature”).

John 3:16 – The central verse

Οὕτως γὰρ ἠγάπησεν ὁ θεὸς τὸν κόσμον, ὥστε τὸν υἱὸν τὸν μονογενῆ ἔδωκεν, ἵνα πᾶς ὁ πιστεύων εἰς αὐτὸν μὴ ἀπόληται ἀλλʼ ἔχῃ ζωὴν αἰώνιον.

Recognisable words: ὁ θεός, τὸν (accusative), ἵνα (so that), πᾶς (all), ὁ (the), πιστεύων (the one who believes), εἰς (into/upon), αὐτόν (him), μή (not, with subjunctive), ἀλλʼ (but), ζωὴν (life accusative).
ζωὴν αἰώνιον = “eternal life”.

Galatians 2:16 – Justification by faith

… οὐ δικαιοῦται ἄνθρωπος ἐξ ἔργων νόμου ἐὰν μὴ διὰ πίστεως Ἰησοῦ Χριστοῦ…

Translation: “…a person is not justified by works of the law but through faith in Jesus Christ / the faithfulness of Jesus Christ.”
The genitive Ἰησοῦ Χριστοῦ can be interpreted as subjective (faith that Christ has) or objective (faith in Christ). This is a key theological debate in Pauline exegesis.

Programmed text · Analysis of John 1:1-5

Apply what you have learned. Answer each question before revealing the answer. This method ensures active comprehension.

Step 1: Identify all the words you know

Ἐν ἀρχῇ ἦν ὁ λόγος, καὶ ὁ λόγος ἦν πρὸς τὸν θεόν, καὶ θεὸς ἦν ὁ λόγος.

Words you know: ἐν (in), ἀρχῇ (beginning – dative), ἦν (was, 3rd sg. imperfect of εἰμί), ὁ (art. nom. masc. sg.), λόγος (word/Word), καί (and), πρός (with, toward + accusative), τόν (art. acc. masc.), θεόν (God acc.), θεός (God nom.).
Translation: “In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God.”
Step 2: What function does ἦν have?

οὗτος ἦν ἐν ἀρχῇ πρὸς τὸν θεόν.

ἦν is the verb “was” (3rd person singular of the imperfect of εἰμί, “to be”). It indicates existence or state in the past with continuous aspect. In this context, it points to the pre‑existence of the Word.
Step 3: Analyse the case of πάντα

πάντα διʼ αὐτοῦ ἐγένετο, καὶ χωρὶς αὐτοῦ ἐγένετο οὐδὲ ἕν.

πάντα is nominative/accusative neuter plural (“all things”). It functions as the subject of ἐγένετο (“came into being”). Translation: “All things were made through him, and without him nothing was made that has been made.”
Step 4: Translate ζωή and φῶς in context

ἐν αὐτῷ ζωὴ ἦν, καὶ ἡ ζωὴ ἦν τὸ φῶς τῶν ἀνθρώπων.

“In him was life, and the life was the light of men.” ζωή = life (eternal, full); φῶς = light (spiritual illumination).
Step 5: What additional meaning does φῶς have in the NT?

καὶ τὸ φῶς ἐν τῇ σκοτίᾳ φαίνει, καὶ ἡ σκοτία αὐτὸ οὐ κατέλαβεν.

“The light shines in the darkness, and the darkness has not overcome it.” φῶς represents Christ as divine revelation that overcomes the darkness of sin and ignorance.
