1. The 24 Greek letters

The Greek alphabet consists of 24 letters, each with its uppercase, lowercase, name, and standard sound (Erasmian system, used in NT studies).

Note on sigma (Σ, σ, ς): The letter sigma has two lowercase forms: σ is used in the middle or at the beginning of a word, and ς (final sigma) is used only at the end of a word. Example: Ἰησοῦς (Jesus).
Practice writing: λόγος (word) – observe the final sigma ς.
ἀγάπη (love) – ends with η, not sigma.
πίστις (faith) – ends with ς (final sigma).
2. Pronunciation, diphthongs, and breathings

Diphthongs (combined sounds)

αι (ai)
ει (ei)
οι (oi)
υι (yi)
αυ (au)
ευ (eu)
ου (ou)
ηυ (ēu)

Improper diphthongs (iota subscript): ᾳ (ᾱι), ῃ (ηι), ῳ (ωι) – pronounced as long α, η, ω respectively.

Breathings (smooth ᾿ and rough ῾)

Every word beginning with a vowel or diphthong carries a breathing. The smooth breathing is not pronounced; the rough breathing is pronounced like an 'h' aspiration. Example: ἀγάπη (agapē) – smooth; ἡμέρα (hēmera) – rough (read "hēmera").

Accents

Greek has three accents: acute (´), grave (`), and circumflex (῀). They indicate pitch accent. In practice, they are all pronounced as a stress accent.

Pronunciation practice: Try reading aloud: θεός (theós), καλός (kalós), λόγος (lógos).
3. Classification of consonants

Greek consonants are classified according to their place of articulation. This is useful for understanding phonetic changes in verbs and nouns.

Labials
π, β, φ
(p, b, ph)
Dentals
τ, δ, θ
(t, d, th)
Velars
κ, γ, χ
(k, g, kh)
Liquids
λ, μ, ν, ρ
(l, m, n, r)
Sibilants
σ, ζ, ξ, ψ
(s, z, ks, ps)
Practical rule: When a stem ends in a labial (π,β,φ) and -σ is added (future, aorist), it forms ψ (e.g. γράφω → γράψω). Velars (κ,γ,χ) + σ → ξ (ἄγω → ἄξω). Dentals (τ,δ,θ) + σ → σ (πείθω → πείσω).

Reading: staurós. Meaning: cross. The word ends in ς (final sigma).
