The course

Why learn Koine Greek?

The New Testament was written in Koine Greek, the common language of the Mediterranean world in the first century. Reading it in its original language allows you to access nuances, structures, and emphases that no translation can fully convey.

This course starts from zero: you will learn the alphabet, phonology, noun and verb morphology, and basic syntax of New Testament Greek — all explained clearly in English.

The 24 lessons of the course

Carefully designed progression: from the alphabet to fluent reading of New Testament texts.

Unit IFoundations of the Greek system
01

The Greek alphabet

Uppercase and lowercase letters, pronunciation, and phonemes of Koine.

 02

Vowels, diphthongs, and accents

Greek accent system: acute, grave, and circumflex.

 03

Introduction to nouns

Grammatical cases, gender, number, and the function of the article.

 04

Second declension

Masculine and neuter nouns ending in -ος and -ον.

 05

First declension

Feminine and masculine nouns ending in -α / -η.

The definite article

Full declension of the article in all genders and cases.
Unit IIThe Greek verb system
07

Present active indicative

Primary active endings. The verb λύω as a model.

 08

Present middle/passive indicative

Middle and passive voice in the present tense. Differences in meaning.

 09

Imperfect indicative

Syllabic and temporal augment. Continuous aspect in the past.

 10

Aorist active indicative

First aorist (weak). Punctual and narrative aspect.

 11

Aorist passive indicative

Passive aorist. Secondary passive endings.

 12

Future indicative

Formation of the future active, middle, and passive.
Unit IIIAdvanced morphology
13

Third declension

Nouns with variable stems. Consonantal and vocalic types.

 14

Greek adjectives

Declension of 2‑termination and 3‑termination adjectives. Attributive and predicative use.

 15

Personal pronouns

ἐγώ, σύ, αὐτός. Emphatic and anaphoric uses.

 16

Relative and demonstrative pronouns

ὅς, ἥ, ὅ · οὗτος · ἐκεῖνος in all cases.

 17

Present participles

Active, middle, and passive participles. Aspect and syntactic functions.

 18

Aorist and perfect participles

Relative time nuances. The genitive absolute participle.

The subjunctive mood

Uses of the subjunctive in purpose clauses, conditions, and exhortations.

 20

The imperative mood

Commands, prohibitions, and entreaties. Difference between present and aorist.

The Greek infinitive

Substantive, complementary, and infinitive clause uses.

Verbs εἰμί and contract verbs

The verb “to be”. Contract verbs in -αω, -εω, -οω.

Comprehensive application: reading and analysis of passages from the Gospel of John.

Methodology

How you will learn

I

Learn the system, not the exception

Greek grammar has a coherent internal logic. Each lesson explains patterns so you can recognize new forms without having seen them before.

II

Strategic vocabulary

The 300 most frequent words in the New Testament cover more than 80% of the text. This course prioritizes this high‑impact vocabulary from the very first lessons.

III

Reading from day one

Each lesson includes real New Testament verses to practice. It’s not grammar for grammar’s sake: everything is aimed at reading the Word in Greek.

IV

Cumulative progression

Each lesson builds on the previous one. By lesson 24 you will have covered 95% of the grammatical structures of the New Testament Greek.
